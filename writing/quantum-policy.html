What Policymakers Should Know About Quantum

2025 · Draft

This post is a draft. Fill in your content below — the structure is here to guide you.

What policymakers usually get wrong

[The two failure modes: overhyping near-term capabilities ("quantum supremacy" headlines) and dismissing long-term risks as too speculative to act on. Neither is useful. Explain why precise timelines are genuinely hard to give — hardware error rates, qubit connectivity, software stack maturity all interact.]

The cryptography threat is real — and time-sensitive

[Harvest now, decrypt later. The window for transitioning encrypted data is longer than most people think — adversaries may already be storing encrypted traffic. NIST's post-quantum cryptography standards (finalized 2024). What federal agencies are required to do and by when. The private sector gap.]

The competitive landscape

[US, China, EU, UK — where the investment is going and what the strategic implications are. The difference between research leadership and industrial capacity. Export controls on quantum hardware and their limits. The workforce pipeline problem.]

What governments should actually do

[Prioritize post-quantum cryptography transition — this has a hard deadline. Fund basic research in hardware — the US has underinvested in manufacturing science. Resist the urge to pick hardware winners (superconducting vs. trapped ion vs. photonic — we do not know yet). Build technical literacy in government — most agencies lack staff who can evaluate vendor claims. Coordinate with allies.]

What to watch

[Key milestones that would actually change the policy calculus: demonstration of logical qubit error rates below threshold, first demonstration of a quantum advantage for a commercially relevant problem, NIST post-quantum migration deadlines, China's hardware progress.]
